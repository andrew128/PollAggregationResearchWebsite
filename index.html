Poll Aggregation

Poll aggregation can be thought of as an extension to polling. Poll aggregators combine multiple polls in a certain way to get a better estimate than a single poll can do. It's like comparing a single data point to results drawn from multiple data points, such as an average.

There are two main types of poll aggregators. The first type are those that look purely at the numbers given from polls with "relatively little additional processing" (PEC). The second type are aggregators that use the data from polls as well as a variety of predicative variables, such as the state of the economy. For example, if a certain demographic has been known to vote for a certain party after certain shifts in the economy, some of these aggregators will account for that. We will refer to aggregators dealing only with polls as "pure aggregators" and predicative aggregators as "predictors".

The goal of my project is to determine whether predictors fare better than pure aggregators in the context of the 2016 Presidential Election and why. I will then try to see if any of the results/explanations can be generalized (senate races, sports competitions, etc.). I chose to cover the 2016 Presidential Election because it was well covered by the poll aggregators.
